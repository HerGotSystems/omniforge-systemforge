🏗 Framework ▾
Problem: Renters pay landlord mortgages forever while being locked out of ownership due to deposit requirements, credit checks, and individual income limits — yet collectively they have massive buying power. Core Mechanism: Cooperative ownership where members contribute monthly payments toward building acquisition and expansion rather than landlord profit. Each property purchased reduces per-person costs and increases collective equity. Leverage Points: — Bulk purchasing drives down per-unit costs by 20–40% — Collective creditworthiness enables financing individuals can't access — Removing landlord profit margin reduces housing costs by 15–25% — Scale economies in maintenance, insurance, legal costs Operating Rule: No member extraction — all surplus goes toward housing more people or reducing member costs, never toward profit-taking by non-residents.
📦 Assets (6) ▾
1. COOPERATIVE FEASIBILITY CALCULATOR Type: calculator Models monthly contributions, acquisition timelines, member equity across scenarios. First use: Input local property prices → see exactly how many people need to pool £1,000/month to buy a building within 12 months. 2. MEMBER VETTING FRAMEWORK Type: template Complete application and screening system: income verification, reference checks, commitment evaluation, exit procedures. First use: Create application form and run first 5 potential members through full screening. 3. LEGAL STRUCTURE BLUEPRINT Type: framework Step-by-step guide to CIC + Housing Cooperative hybrid. Articles of Association template, member agreement, solicitor brief. First use: Book consultation with cooperative housing solicitor using the provided brief. 4. PROPERTY PIPELINE SYSTEM Type: tool Rightmove API + scoring algorithm: identifies multi-unit properties under £300k, calculates renovation costs, flags legal issues. First use: Set up alerts for 3–6 unit properties and score your first 10 acquisitions. 5. BANK PITCH DECK TEMPLATE Type: template Presentation designed for cooperative housing — risk mitigation, comparable models, member commitment evidence, financial projections. First use: Customise with local market data. Book 3 building society mortgage advisers. 6. GOVERNANCE PLAYBOOK Type: playbook Meeting structures, decision protocols, conflict resolution, financial transparency templates. First use: Run your first formal member meeting using the agenda template.
🧪 Experiments (5) ▾
1. MICRO-POOL VALIDATION Hypothesis: If 10 people commit £1,000/month for 6 months, banks will engage — because they respond to demonstrated cash flow over promises. Method: Ring-fenced account → month 3, approach 3 building societies with £30,000 cash + 10 committed members. Time: 6 months Success = Mortgage pre-approval on actual property Fail signal = No bank will consider lending 2. LEGAL STRUCTURE TEST Hypothesis: Correct cooperative legal entity → banks treat us as viable borrowers. Method: CIC + Housing Coop hybrid via solicitor. Get 3 banks to review and provide preliminary lending criteria. Time: 8 weeks Success = 1 bank confirms they will lend to this structure Fail signal = No banks will lend OR legal costs exceed £5,000 3. MEMBER COMMITMENT VALIDATION Hypothesis: 12-month contracts + financial screening filters serious members. Method: Recruit 25 people, run full vetting with financial checks and contracts. Time: 3 months Success = 15+ pass screening + first payments collected Fail signal = Less than 60% pass or high drop-out after first payment
🚀 Pilot Plan — 30 days ▾
Goal: Prove 15 people will commit £1,000/month and identify first target property. Week 1 — Legal foundation — Meet cooperative housing solicitor — Draft CIC articles — Open business banking account Week 2 — Member recruitment — Launch website with calculator + application — Host 2 info sessions (online + in-person) — Target: 25 applications Week 3 — Property pipeline — Set up Rightmove alerts — Contact 5 estate agents about multi-unit properties — Score 10 potential properties, shortlist 3 Week 4 — Commitment validation — Complete member vetting — Hold member meeting to review shortlisted properties — Collect first payments, book bank meetings First Revenue Path: Month 6 → £60,000 pooled → purchase first property → members at £800/month → Month 7 revenue: £8,000 contributions + £800 net surplus. Day 15 checkpoint: 15 applications + 3 properties scored + legal 50% done + bank meeting booked.
📋 System Sheet ▾
Problem in one line: Renters pay landlords forever while excluded from ownership due to individual financial limits. Solution in one line: Groups pool £1,000/month to buy buildings collectively, house members at cost, reinvest surplus to expand. Biggest risk (honest): Banks may refuse cooperative structures, or members leave after purchase, leaving others holding unsustainable debt. Success in 12 months: 3 properties housing 45 members + £15,000/month collective revenue + 30+ person waiting list. Do NOT build this if: You can't get 10+ people to commit real money upfront, or local prices make the math impossible, or you're not ready for 2+ years of complex legal and financial work.

// FAQ

Common Questions

What's an Anthropic API key and how do I get one? +
Anthropic makes the Claude AI that powers Omniforge. To get a key, go to console.anthropic.com, sign up, and create an API key. Add a few pounds of credit — a Deep forge costs about £0.03–0.05. Your key is stored only in your browser, never on our servers.
What's the difference between Quick, Standard, and Deep? +
Quick gives you 3 assets, 3 experiments, 7-day pilot. Standard gives 5 assets, 4 experiments, 14-day pilot. Deep adds all of that plus a full Deployment Layer — practitioner pipeline, 3 economics models, tech stack tiers (£0/£50/£200+ per month), quality framework, and 36-month scaling roadmap.
Is my idea safe? Does Omniforge store my inputs? +
Your forge history is stored in your browser's local storage only — it never hits our servers. Your API key is also local only. We have no database of your ideas. The only data that leaves your browser is what goes directly to Anthropic's API.
What kind of ideas work best? +
Anything with a clear problem it's trying to solve. Communities, products, services, platforms, internal tools, learning programs, local projects, creative businesses — the more specific your idea and risk, the better the output. Vague input produces vague output.
Can I use this for existing projects, not just new ideas? +
Absolutely. Describe your existing project as the idea, tell it your domain and audience, and set the risk as "what's blocking us right now." The experiments and pilot sections are especially useful for projects that are stuck.
